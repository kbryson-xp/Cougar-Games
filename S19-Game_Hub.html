How to play

▼

About this game

Tic-Tac-Toe is one of the oldest games still played today. The earliest known three-in-a-row boards are scratched into roof tiles in ancient Egypt around 1300 BCE, and the Romans played a close cousin called Terni Lapilli ("three pebbles at a time"), with their grids found chalked on walls and monuments all over Rome.

The English name "Tic-Tac-Toe" actually came from a totally different children's game involving slates and pencils — historians think the name jumped over to the noughts-and-crosses grid in the United States during the 20th century. In most other countries it's still called Noughts and Crosses or Xs and Os.

It's also a piece of computer-science history: in 1952, a working Tic-Tac-Toe program called OXO became one of the very first video games ever made.

Goal

Get three of your marks in a row — across, down, or diagonally.

How to play

  1. Player 1 (Paw) goes first.
  2. Click any empty square to place your mark.
  3. Take turns until someone wins or the board fills up.

Winning

Line up three in a row — horizontal, vertical, or diagonal. If the board fills with no winner, it's a draw.

Winning lines

Strategy tip

If you go first, the center is the strongest opening. Corners are second-best. The middle of an edge is the weakest spot.

How to play

▼

About this game

Four in a Row — better known by its original trademarked name Connect Four — is a fairly young game. It was designed by American toy inventor Howard Wexler and published by Milton Bradley in 1974. Wexler's idea was simple but unusual: take strategy games off the table and stand them up vertically, so gravity becomes part of the game.

It caught on slowly at first, then exploded after Milton Bradley's "Pretty Sneaky, Sis" TV commercial aired in 1977. The game has gone by lots of names around the world — Captain's Mistress, Find Four, Plot Four, and (in the former Soviet Union) Gravitrips.

One unusual fact: it's a solved game. In 1988, computer scientists proved that the first player can always win with perfect play by starting in the center column. Most humans don't play perfectly though, which is why it's still fun.

Goal

Be the first to connect four of your discs in a row.

How to play

  1. Player 1 (Teal) goes first.
  2. Click a column number to drop your disc into that column.
  3. The disc falls to the lowest empty space.
  4. Take turns dropping discs.

Winning

Connect four discs in a line — horizontally, vertically, or diagonally. The winning line will glow.

Win directions

Strategy tip

Watch out for "trap" moves where your opponent can win in two different ways at once. Playing in the center column first gives you the most winning options.

How to play

▼

About this game

Checkers descends from a much older Egyptian game called Alquerque, played as far back as 1400 BCE on a 5×5 grid. Pieces and boards resembling Alquerque have been found in Egyptian temples and ancient tombs.

Around 1100 CE, a clever Frenchman moved the game onto a 64-square chessboard and gave it 12 pieces per side — and that's essentially the version we still play today. The French called it Fierges; in England it became "Draughts," and Americans renamed it "Checkers."

The game has been a milestone in computer science too. In 1952, IBM scientist Arthur Samuel wrote one of the first programs that could actually learn from playing Checkers — an early ancestor of today's machine learning. And in 2007, Checkers became the most complicated game ever fully solved by computers: with perfect play on both sides, the game is always a draw.

Goal

Capture all of your opponent's pieces, or leave them with no legal moves.

The board

An 8x8 board. Each player starts with 12 pieces on the dark squares of the three rows closest to them.

How to move

  1. Click your piece to select it. The squares you can move to will glow.
  2. Click a glowing square to move there.
  3. Pieces move diagonally forward, one square at a time.

Jumping (capturing)

If your opponent's piece is diagonally next to yours, and the square just past it is empty, you can jump over their piece to capture it.

A jump in action
Teal jumps over gold and lands in the empty square

You MUST jump

If you can jump, you have to! Pieces with available jumps will glow brighter than other pieces. Only those pieces will be selectable.

Multi-jumps

If after a jump you can jump again, you must keep jumping with the same piece until you can't. This can let you capture multiple pieces in one turn!

Kings

When one of your pieces reaches the far end of the board, it becomes a King — marked with a crown. Kings can move and jump both forward and backward.

Special rule: If your jump lands you on the back row and you become a King, your turn ends right there — no further multi-jumps that turn.

Winning

You win when your opponent has no pieces left, or has no legal moves.

Strategy tip

Try to keep your back row intact for as long as possible — this prevents your opponent from kinging. And remember: multi-jumps win games — set them up when you can!

Reversi

How to play

▼

About this game

Reversi was invented in England in the 1880s. Two Englishmen — Lewis Waterman and John Mollett — both claimed credit, each accusing the other of stealing the idea. Whichever of them really came first, the version that survived was Waterman's, played on the 8×8 board we still use today.

The game faded in popularity for decades, until 1971, when a Japanese salesman named Goro Hasegawa rediscovered it, made some small rule tweaks, and renamed it Othello — after Shakespeare's play, because the constantly flipping black and white pieces reminded him of the play's dramatic reversals of fortune. Hasegawa's version became a worldwide hit, selling more than 40 million copies.

So Reversi and Othello are essentially the same game, with Othello being the standardized tournament version. We use the older name out of respect for its English inventors.

Goal

Have the most discs of your color on the board when the game ends.

The board

An 8x8 grid. The game starts with 4 discs in the center in a checkered pattern.

Starting position

How to play

  1. Player 1 (Teal) goes first.
  2. You can only place a disc on an empty square that flanks at least one opponent disc.
  3. "Flank" means your new disc and another of your discs sandwich a line of opponent discs in any direction.
  4. All flanked opponent discs flip to your color!

What "flanking" looks like

You can flank in any of 8 directions: up, down, left, right, and the four diagonals. A single move can flip discs in multiple directions at once.

Example flank
Teal places on the right → all 3 gold discs flip to teal

Helpful hint

The board shows faint dots on every square where you can legally play. If there are no dots, your turn will be skipped!

Skipping turns

If you can't make any legal move, your turn is automatically skipped and your opponent goes again.

Ending the game

The game ends when neither player can move (usually when the board is full). Whoever has more discs wins!

Strategy tip

The corners are very powerful — once a disc is in a corner, it can never be flipped. Try to grab corners and avoid playing squares right next to corners (those let your opponent reach the corner).

Player 2 (Top)

How to play

▼

About this game

Mancala is one of the oldest families of games in the world. The word "mancala" comes from the Arabic naqala, meaning "to move." It isn't really one game but hundreds of related "sowing and capture" games spread across Africa, the Middle East, and Asia — all built around picking up seeds or stones from one pit and dropping them one at a time into the next pits around the board.

The earliest mancala-style boards we've found are carved into stone in ancient Egyptian temples, with examples dating from before 1400 BCE, and possibly even earlier examples in Jordan. The game traveled across Africa and out to the rest of the world along trade routes carried by Arab merchants.

Mancala was also brought to the Americas by enslaved Africans during the trans-Atlantic slave trade, where it kept being played as a way to preserve community and culture. The version most commonly played in the United States today is called Kalah.

Goal

Be the player with the most stones in your store when the game ends.

The board

Each player has 6 pits in front of them and a big store on their right side. Player 1 sits at the bottom; Player 2 sits at the top.

Board layout

How to play

  1. On your turn, click one of your own pits (Player 1: bottom row; Player 2: top row).
  2. All the stones from that pit are picked up and "sown" counterclockwise, one per pit.
  3. If you pass your own store, drop a stone in it. Skip your opponent's store.

Bonus turn

If your last stone lands in your own store, you take another turn!

Capture

If your last stone lands in an empty pit on your side, and the pit directly across has stones, you capture both that stone and all stones across — they go in your store.

Ending the game

The game ends when one player's side is empty. The other player moves all their remaining stones into their own store. Whoever has the most stones wins!

Strategy tip

Try to set up bonus turns by ending your sow exactly in your store. Multiple bonus turns in a row can completely change the game.

How to play

▼

About this game

Solitaire — known as "Patience" in most of the world — first appears in print in a German game book in 1788. Its true birthplace is debated; historians suspect it grew out of card-based fortune-telling games in Scandinavia, Germany, or France in the late 1700s. A popular legend (probably more myth than fact) says it was invented by a French nobleman in prison during the French Revolution, with nothing but a deck of cards to pass the time.

The version we play here, called Klondike, gets its name from the Klondike Gold Rush in Canada in the 1890s. The story goes that miners played it during long, cold downtime in the Yukon. Klondike became the most popular variant by far — so much so that "Solitaire" usually just means Klondike now.

Most people first met Solitaire on a computer. In 1990, Microsoft included it in Windows 3.0, originally as a way to teach new users how to use a mouse — click, drag, and drop. It became the most-used Windows application for years and helped make the game a global phenomenon.

Goal

Move all 52 cards to the four foundation piles (top right) — sorted by suit, in order from Ace to King.

The layout

  • Tableau (bottom): 7 columns where you arrange cards. Only the top card of each column starts face-up.
  • Foundations (top right): 4 empty piles. Build up from Ace to King, one suit each.
  • Stock & Waste (top left): The deck of remaining cards. Click the stock to flip cards over.

Moving cards in the tableau

You can move a card onto another card in a tableau column if:

  • The new card is the opposite color (red on black, black on red), and
  • The new card is one rank lower.

For example: a black 7 can go on a red 8.

Moving stacks

You can move a whole stack of face-up cards from one column to another, as long as the bottom card of the stack follows the rule above.

Empty columns

Only a King (or a stack starting with a King) can be placed on an empty tableau column.

Foundations

Move cards to a foundation in this order: Ace, 2, 3, ... 10, Jack, Queen, King. Each foundation must be the same suit.

Tip: Click a face-up card to instantly send it to the foundation if it fits!

Flipping cards

When you move a card off the top of a face-down card in the tableau, the face-down card flips automatically.

The stock pile

Click the stock to flip the top card to the waste pile. The top of the waste can be played anywhere it fits. When the stock is empty, click the empty spot to recycle the waste back into the stock.

Draw 1 vs. Draw 3

Use the toggle above the board to switch between draw modes:

  • Draw 1: One card flips at a time. Easier — most cards become available.
  • Draw 3: Three cards flip at once, but only the top one can be played. Harder, more strategic — the classic challenge.

Winning

You win when all 52 cards are on the foundations!

Strategy tip

Don't rush to send cards to the foundation — sometimes you'll need a low card later to help organize the tableau. Try to flip face-down cards early to give yourself more options.

Sternhalma

Player 2 (Gold)
Player 1's turn
Teal home 0/10

How to play

▼

About this game

This game was invented in Germany in 1892 as Stern-Halma — German for "Star Halma." Stern means star, named for the six-pointed board. It was a variant of an older American game called Halma (Greek for "jump"), invented in 1883.

When the game came to the United States in the late 1920s, the publisher renamed it "Chinese Checkers" — a marketing tactic to make it sound exotic. But the game has no connection to China, and it isn't actually checkers either.

We're using its real name out of respect for accurate history. You may also hear it called "Star Halma" or its more common American name.

Goal

Be the first player to move all 10 of your pieces from your starting triangle into your opponent's triangle (directly across the star).

The board

A six-pointed star. Player 1 (Teal) starts at the bottom; Player 2 (Gold) starts at the top. Each player wants to fill the triangle on the opposite side.

How to move

  1. Click your piece to select it. The spaces it can move to will glow.
  2. Click a glowing space to move there.
  3. Each turn, you can either step or hop — but not both.

Stepping

Move to any empty space directly next to your piece (in any of 6 directions). Your turn ends after a single step.

Hopping

Jump over a piece (yours OR your opponent's) to land in the empty space on the other side. The piece is not captured — it stays where it is.

You can chain hops! After one hop, if the same piece can hop again, you can keep hopping for as long as you want (or stop and end your turn).

Important rule

Once a piece reaches the opponent's home triangle, it cannot leave. It can still move within the home, just not back out.

Winning

Win by having all 10 of your pieces in your opponent's home triangle.

Strategy tip

Look for hop chains — a single turn with several jumps can move a piece across the board very quickly. Try to build "ladders" of pieces that help your pieces leapfrog forward.

How to play

About this game

Backgammon is one of the oldest games still played. Its earliest known ancestor is the Royal Game of Ur, played in ancient Mesopotamia (modern-day Iraq) around 3000 BCE — about 5,000 years ago. Boards and dice for these games have been found in royal tombs, with playing pieces made of shell, bone, and lapis lazuli.

The Romans played a close cousin called Ludus Duodecim Scriptorum ("the game of twelve lines"), which evolved over centuries into the Greek game Tabula. Tabula already had most of the rules we use now: rolling two dice, racing pieces around the board, and hitting opposing pieces.

The English word "backgammon" first appeared around 1650, and the modern rules were written down by Edmund Hoyle in his 1743 book A Short Treatise on the Game of Backgammon. Aside from a few small additions like the doubling cube, the game you're playing here is essentially the one Hoyle described — and the one Roman emperors and ancient Sumerian kings would still recognize.

Goal

Be the first player to bear off (remove) all 15 of your checkers from the board.

Setup

Each player has 15 checkers in the standard starting arrangement. White moves counter-clockwise (high numbers toward low). Black moves clockwise (low numbers toward high).

Your turn

  1. Click Roll dice.
  2. Click a die to choose which value to use.
  3. Click one of your checkers — it moves that many points in your direction.
  4. Repeat for each remaining die.

Doubles

Roll the same number on both dice and you get four moves of that value instead of two.

Hits and the bar

Landing on a point with exactly one opposing checker sends it to the bar. A player with checkers on the bar must re-enter them in the opponent's home before making any other moves. A point with two or more opposing checkers is blocked.

Bearing off

Once all 15 of your checkers are in your home board, you can start bearing them off. Roll the value of a point you occupy to remove a checker from there. If you roll higher than your highest occupied point, you may bear off from that highest point.

Assisted vs Classic mode

Assisted highlights legal source checkers and destinations to help you learn. Classic hides those hints — true Backgammon, all on you.

Chess

The classic game of strategy. Checkmate the opposing king to win.
White
Black
White's turn

How to play

About this game

Chess began in northern India around the 6th century CE as a game called Chaturanga. The Sanskrit word means "four divisions of the military" — infantry, cavalry, elephants, and chariots — which became the modern pawns, knights, bishops, and rooks. It was a war game, designed to teach battlefield strategy.

Chaturanga spread to Persia, where it became Shatranj. The Persians gave us several words we still use today: when their King was trapped, they would shout "Shāh māt!" ("the king is helpless") — the origin of the English word "checkmate." Arab traders carried the game across North Africa to Spain, and it spread through Europe over the next several hundred years.

The version we play today took shape in 15th-century Spain and Italy, when the Queen and Bishop became the powerful long-range pieces they are now. (Before that, the Queen could only move one square at a time.) Chess has been played, written about, and studied without interruption for more than 1,500 years — making it one of the longest-lived games in human history.

Goal

Put the opposing King in checkmate — a position where the king is attacked and cannot escape, block, or capture the attacker.

How pieces move

  • Pawn — moves forward one square (or two from its starting square). Captures diagonally one square forward.
  • Rook — any number of squares in straight lines (horizontal or vertical).
  • Bishop — any number of squares diagonally.
  • Queen — like a rook and bishop combined; any direction, any distance.
  • Knight — L-shape: two squares one direction then one square perpendicular. Jumps over pieces.
  • King — one square in any direction.

Special moves

Castling: if the king and a rook haven't moved and the squares between are empty (and the king isn't in or moving through check), the king moves two squares toward the rook and the rook hops to the king's other side.

En passant: if your opponent's pawn moves two squares forward and lands beside your pawn, you may capture it as if it had only moved one square — but only on your very next move.

Promotion: when your pawn reaches the far end of the board, you can choose to promote it to a Queen, Rook, Bishop, or Knight.

Your turn

  1. Click one of your pieces.
  2. Click a square to move there.
  3. If you change your mind, click your piece again to deselect.

Check, checkmate, and draws

If your king is attacked, you're in check and must get out of it. Checkmate ends the game. Games can also end in a draw by stalemate (no legal moves but not in check), threefold repetition, the 50-move rule, or insufficient material.

Assisted vs Classic mode

Assisted highlights legal destinations when you select a piece. Classic hides those hints — true chess, all on you.

Go

The ancient game of territory. Surround empty space and capture enemy stones to score the most points.
Black 0
White 0
Black's turn

How to play

About this game

Go is the oldest board game still played in its original form. It was invented in China sometime between 3,000 and 4,000 years ago, where it's called Weiqi (圍棋), meaning "the surrounding game." Legend says Emperor Yao created it to teach his unruly son discipline and concentration — though the first written record is in a Chinese history book from 548 BCE, which mentions Go as already long-established.

By the time of Confucius (around 500 BCE), Go was considered one of the "Four Accomplishments" every educated person should master, alongside calligraphy, painting, and music. The game spread to Korea, where it's called Baduk, and to Japan around the 7th century CE, where it became Igo and was so respected that the shogun supported professional Go schools whose top player held a cabinet-level government position.

Today Go is enormous in East Asia — played by tens of millions of people. In 2016, Go made global headlines when Google's AI program AlphaGo defeated world champion Lee Sedol, a moment many consider one of the most important milestones in artificial intelligence so far.

Goal

Score the most points by surrounding territory (empty intersections) and capturing your opponent's stones. This is a beginner-friendly 9×9 board — full Go is played on 19×19, but the rules are identical.

How to play

  1. Black goes first. Players take turns placing one stone on any empty intersection (not the squares — the dots where lines cross).
  2. Once placed, stones don't move. They stay where they're put.
  3. You can also pass instead of placing a stone. Two passes in a row ends the game.

Liberties and capture

An empty intersection next to a stone (up/down/left/right — not diagonal) is called a liberty. Stones of the same color that touch each other form a group and share their liberties.

If a group has zero liberties, every stone in it is captured and removed from the board. Each captured stone is one point for the capturing side.

Two important rules

Suicide: you can't place a stone that would leave your own group with zero liberties — unless that same move captures enemy stones first.

Ko: you can't make a move that recreates the exact board position from your last turn. This stops infinite back-and-forth recaptures of a single stone.

Ending and scoring

The game ends when both players pass in a row. Then we count area score:

  • Each of your stones still on the board = 1 point
  • Each empty intersection completely surrounded by only your stones = 1 point
  • White also gets komi (a 6.5-point bonus) to make up for going second

Highest score wins. The 0.5 komi means there are no ties.

Assisted vs Classic mode

Assisted marks illegal moves with a red ✕ (suicide and ko) and shows the last move with a small dot. Classic only shows the last-move dot — you have to spot illegal moves on your own.

Tips for your first games

  • Corners and sides are easier to surround than the center — start there.
  • Two separate "eyes" (empty spaces inside a group) make a group alive forever — it can never be captured.
  • It's okay to pass when you can't think of a useful move. Don't fill in your own territory!

Gomoku

Five in a row wins. Take turns placing stones — first to line up five horizontally, vertically, or diagonally takes it.
Black
White
Black's turn

How to play

About this game

Gomoku grew out of Go. Once Go-style boards and stones were widely available across East Asia, players started inventing simpler games to play with the same equipment — and "five in a row" was the most popular of them all. The earliest reliable historical records of Gomoku as a distinct game come from Japan during the Edo period in the mid-1700s.

The name comes from Japanese: gomoku narabe (五目並べ) means "five points in a row." Go means five, moku is a counter for pieces, and narabe means line-up. The same game is called Wuziqi in China and Omok in Korea — all variations on "five stones."

Japanese players were the first to notice that the player who goes first has a built-in advantage. To balance this, they developed a stricter tournament version called Renju ("connected pearls"), which adds rules limiting what Black is allowed to do. The version here is the simpler casual rules — fun for beginners, no restrictions.

Goal

Be the first to get five of your stones in a row — horizontally, vertically, or diagonally.

How to play

  1. Black goes first. Players take turns placing one stone on any empty intersection (the dots where lines cross — not the squares).
  2. Once placed, stones don't move and aren't captured. They stay where they're put.
  3. The first player to line up exactly five (or more) of their stones in a straight unbroken line wins.

What "in a row" means

Five stones of the same color, all touching, in a straight line:

  • Horizontal — five stones across one row
  • Vertical — five stones down one column
  • Diagonal — five stones along either diagonal

The line must be unbroken — no gaps and no opponent stones in between.

Tips for your first games

  • Watch for open threes — three of your opponent's stones in a row with empty space on both ends. If you don't block, they'll have two ways to make four, and you can only block one of them.
  • Try to make threats while blocking. A move that defends and builds your own line is twice as good.
  • Going first is a slight advantage — Black has a small edge with perfect play. If you're new, take Black to start; once you're better, swap.

Draws

If the entire 15×15 board fills up without anyone making five in a row, the game is a draw. This is rare — usually someone wins long before the board is full.

Assisted vs Classic mode

Assisted shows the last move with a small dot, and highlights the winning five with a glowing line at the end. Classic shows only the winning line — no last-move dot. You're tracking the position yourself.

How to play

About this game

Dominoes is one of the oldest tile games still played widely. The earliest known sets come from China around the 12th century, where the tiles represented the 21 outcomes of throwing two dice. The European version we play today — using 28 tiles numbered 0–6 on each half — appeared in Italy in the 1700s and spread quickly through France, Spain, and the Caribbean. Today every region has its own favorite rules; this version is Draw Dominoes, the most common casual rule set.

Goal

Empty your hand before your opponent, or be holding the fewest pips when the game blocks. Win rounds to score points; the first player to reach 100 points wins the match.

The set

A standard double-six set has 28 tiles. Each tile shows two numbers from 0 (blank) to 6 — every possible pair, including doubles like 6-6.

Setup

  1. Each player draws 7 tiles. The remaining 14 tiles form the boneyard.
  2. The player with the highest double (6-6, then 5-5, etc.) plays it first. If neither has a double, the highest-pip tile leads.
  3. That tile becomes the start of the chain.

How to play a tile

  • On your turn, play one tile that matches a number on either open end of the chain. Click an open end to play your selected tile there.
  • Doubles are placed crosswise (perpendicular to the chain) — but they only count one side as an open end.
  • If you can't play, draw from the boneyard until you get a playable tile. If the boneyard is empty and you still can't play, you must pass.

Ending a round

A round ends one of two ways:

  • Domino! — A player plays their last tile. They win the round and score the total pips in the loser's hand.
  • Blocked — Boneyard is empty and neither player can play. The player with the fewer pips in hand wins; their score increases by the difference between the two hands.

Winning the match

Scores carry across rounds. After each round, the loser of that round leads the next one (or the winner of a tied block). First player to reach 100 points wins the match.

Tips for your first games

  • Watch which numbers your opponent can't play — if they keep drawing on a 5, they probably don't have one. Try to keep both ends as 5s to force more draws.
  • Don't be in a hurry to play your doubles. Doubles are easy to get stuck with at the end of a round, where they'll cost you points.
  • Count the pips in your hand. If a round looks like it's going to block, you want to be holding less than your opponent.

Open vs Hidden hands

Hidden is the standard way dominoes is played: each player's tiles are face-down to the other, and you pass the device when it's the other player's turn. You have to figure out which of your tiles can be played yourself — illegal clicks shake the tile. Open is a friendlier learning mode: both hands stay face-up the whole game, and tiles you can't play are dimmed for you. Great for first-time players and anyone wanting a casual round.

Nine Men's Morris

Place pieces, then slide them along lines to make rows of three. Every "mill" you form lets you remove an opponent's piece.
Player 19

Player 1 to place

How to play

About this game

Nine Men's Morris is one of the oldest board games still played anywhere. Boards have been found carved into the roof tiles of an Egyptian temple from around 1400 BCE, scratched into stone benches in the Roman Forum, and etched into the cloister seats of medieval cathedrals all across Europe — apparently bored monks loved it. The game has gone by many names: Mill, Merels, Mills, Mühle, Müllespiel. Shakespeare even mentions it in A Midsummer Night's Dream: "the nine-men's-morris is filled up with mud."

It was the chess of its era — a thinking person's game played by everyone from peasants to kings — and it's still popular today in parts of Europe and Africa. The rules are simple, but good play takes real planning.

Goal

Reduce your opponent to just two pieces, or trap them so they can't make any legal move. Either way, you win.

The board

Three nested squares connected by short lines. There are 24 spots where pieces can sit — the corners and the midpoints of each square's sides. Pieces only move along the printed lines.

Phase 1 — Placing

  1. Players take turns placing one of their nine pieces on any empty spot.
  2. Player 1 goes first. Keep placing until both players have used all nine pieces.
  3. If you place a piece that completes a mill (three of your pieces in a straight line on the board), you immediately remove one of your opponent's pieces from the board — see "Mills" below.

Phase 2 — Moving

  1. Once all eighteen pieces are placed, players take turns sliding one of their pieces along a line to an empty adjacent spot.
  2. You can only move to a spot that's directly connected by a line to where your piece is now. Pieces can't jump over other pieces.
  3. If a slide forms a mill, you remove one of your opponent's pieces, just like in the placing phase.

Mills — the heart of the game

A mill is three of your own pieces in an unbroken straight line along one of the printed lines on the board. Each mill you form (whether by placing or by moving) lets you take one of your opponent's pieces off the board for good.

When you have to remove an opponent's piece:

  • You cannot remove a piece that is part of a mill — unless every one of your opponent's pieces is in a mill, in which case you can pick any of them.
  • Removed pieces are gone for the rest of the game.

You can also break and re-form a mill: move a piece out of a mill on one turn, then back in on a later turn, and that counts as a fresh mill — you get to remove another opponent's piece. Reusing mills like this is one of the main strategies.

Phase 3 — Flying

When you're reduced to just three pieces, the rules loosen up to give you a fighting chance: instead of sliding to an adjacent spot, you can "fly" to any empty spot on the board. This makes mills much easier to form and turns the endgame into a tense scramble.

How you win

  • Reduce your opponent to two pieces. With only two pieces left, they can't possibly form a mill, so you've won.
  • Trap your opponent so they have no legal move on their turn. (This is rarer but absolutely allowed.)

Tips for your first games

  • The center spots of each side (the ones with four lines coming out of them) are the most valuable — they're part of the most lines, so they help you make the most mills.
  • Watch for your opponent's "swing mills" — two parallel mills they can break and reform alternately. Once they set one of these up, they'll keep capturing your pieces every turn unless you can block.
  • Don't bunch your pieces — spread out so you're threatening multiple mills at once.

Assisted vs Classic mode

Assisted highlights legal targets when you pick up a piece, glows gold when you've formed a mill, and marks which opponent pieces you're allowed to capture. Classic shows none of that — you do all the tracking yourself, and illegal clicks just shake. Pick whichever matches the level of challenge you want.

How to play

About this game

Yacht is the great-grandfather of an entire family of five-dice scoring games. Sailors, ranchers, and travelers in the 1800s played it under names like Yacht, Generala, Cheerio, and Poker Dice — all variations on the same idea: roll five dice, hold what you like, re-roll the rest, and try to score combinations on a fixed scorecard. The Scandinavian version (Yatzy) is still wildly popular today. The most famous American descendant was supposedly invented in the 1940s by a wealthy Canadian couple who played the game on their yacht with friends, then paid a game maker named Edwin Lowe to print up boxes as gifts. Lowe bought the rights, marketed it nationally, and the game became one of the best-selling dice games of all time. We're playing the original — Yacht.

Goal

Score more points than your opponent across thirteen rounds. Each round you roll five dice up to three times, then commit the result to one of thirteen scoring categories — and each category can only be used once per player.

A turn, step by step

  1. Roll all five dice. That's your first roll.
  2. Hold any dice you want to keep. Tap a die to set it aside; tap again to release it.
  3. Roll the rest. You can re-roll the un-held dice up to two more times (three rolls total).
  4. Commit to a category. When you're done rolling — or after the third roll — pick any unused category in your column. The score for those dice in that category is locked in. Your turn ends.

You don't have to use all three rolls. If your first roll is already great, you can score it right away.

Scoring categories

Upper section — score the sum of dice matching the face. (Threes with three 3s and a pair of 5s = 9.)

  • Ones through Sixes — sum of dice showing that number.

Upper bonus — if your upper section totals at least 63, you earn a +35 bonus. (63 is the magic number: three of each face. Every "extra" die above the average gets you closer.)

Lower section:

  • Three of a Kind — at least three matching dice. Score: sum of all five dice.
  • Four of a Kind — at least four matching dice. Score: sum of all five dice.
  • Full House — three of one face and two of another. Score: 25.
  • Small Straight — four dice in a row (1-2-3-4, 2-3-4-5, or 3-4-5-6). Score: 30.
  • Large Straight — five dice in a row (1-2-3-4-5 or 2-3-4-5-6). Score: 40.
  • Yacht — all five dice the same. Score: 50.
  • Chance — anything goes. Score: sum of all five dice. This is your safety net for bad rolls.

Yacht bonus

If you've already scored 50 in your Yacht box and you roll another Yacht, you earn a +100 bonus automatically — and you still have to score that turn somewhere. (If your Yacht box is empty when you roll a Yacht, of course score it for 50!) If your Yacht box was already used and is worth zero, no bonus.

For your placement on a bonus Yacht: if the upper-section box for that face is open, you must use it. Otherwise you may use any open lower-section box at its face value (Full House = 25, Small Straight = 30, Large Straight = 40, etc.). If only upper-section boxes are open, score 0 there.

Forced zeros

You don't have to score a good result — every turn you must commit somewhere, even if it means taking a zero in a category you don't want. Late in the game when you're running out of empty boxes, this matters. Pick your sacrifice wisely.

The game ends

After both players have filled all thirteen categories. Add up upper section + bonus + lower section + any Yacht bonuses. Highest total wins.

Tips for your first games

  • Chase the upper bonus. Three of each face hits 63 exactly. Going for the bonus is usually worth more than chasing a marginal full house.
  • Save Chance for a junk roll. Don't burn it on a 22 if there's no upper-section payoff first.
  • Yachts feel rare but happen. If you roll three or four of a kind on your first roll, holding for the Yacht is often the right call.
  • Take the small straight if it's there. Don't gamble it for the large straight unless you have a sure shot — small straight = 30 is solid.
  • Sacrifice early. If you're going to take a zero somewhere, do it on Ones or Yacht early — those are the ones most likely to be hopeless anyway.

Assisted vs Classic mode

Assisted shows you what each category would score if you picked it now — categories that would score zero are dimmed, and your highest-scoring legal pick is highlighted gold. Classic shows nothing — you decide what to score using your own judgment, and once you click a row that's it. (You're still allowed to take a zero in any open category in either mode — that's part of Yacht.)

How to play

About this game

Naval guessing games like this one started life as pencil-and-paper games in the early 1900s — sailors and soldiers in the World War I era played versions called Salvo, Broadsides, and L'Attaque Navale on grid paper, calling out coordinates to "fire" at their friend's hidden fleet. The plastic-peg version most people know came along in the 1960s, and computerized versions starting in 1979 were among the first video games sold for home computers.

It's a game of pure deduction with a streak of luck — no actual board to look at, just your memory of every shot you've taken and a guess at where your opponent's ships might be hiding.

Goal

Sink all five of your opponent's ships before they sink yours.

The fleet

Each player has the same five ships, totaling 17 cells:

  • Carrier — 5 cells
  • Battleship — 4 cells
  • Cruiser — 3 cells
  • Submarine — 3 cells
  • Destroyer — 2 cells

Setup — placing your ships

  1. You'll see a 10×10 grid labeled with letters A–J across the top and numbers 1–10 down the side.
  2. Click a ship in the roster on the right, then click any cell on your grid to drop the ship's first square there. The rest of the ship extends from that cell.
  3. Click the Rotate button (or press R) to switch between horizontal and vertical placement.
  4. Ships can't overlap and can't go off the edge of the grid. The preview turns red if your placement is illegal.
  5. Click Random to auto-place your whole fleet. This replaces any ships you've already placed, so use it as a starting point or to start over.
  6. Once all five ships are placed, click Ready to start firing.

Playing

  1. On your turn, click a cell on the tracking grid (the right-hand grid that shows your shots so far). You're calling out that coordinate to your opponent.
  2. Miss — the cell turns dark with a white dot. You missed.
  3. Hit — the cell turns red with an X. You hit one of their ships.
  4. Sunk — when you hit the last cell of a ship, all of that ship's cells turn dark red, and the fleet tracker shows it crossed off.
  5. Then it's the other player's turn.

Two-player on one device

When playing two humans on the same Chromebook, a privacy curtain covers the screen between turns. The current player taps "I'm ready" to start their turn, takes their shot, and then the curtain comes back up so the other player doesn't see what just happened on their fleet.

Playing against the computer

Pick a difficulty:

  • Easy — fires totally at random. The computer doesn't even remember its hits. Good for younger players or first games.
  • Medium — fires randomly until it gets a hit, then attacks adjacent cells until the ship sinks. Plays like a careful human.
  • Hard — same as Medium, but in random-shot mode it only checks every other cell (like a checkerboard). That's the optimal hunting pattern, and it'll find your ships much faster.

Tips for your first games

  • Don't bunch your ships in a corner. Spread them out so a single hit doesn't immediately reveal the others.
  • When you get a hit, work the diagonals. Ships are straight lines, so once you've hit two cells in a row you know which direction to keep firing.
  • Keep your shots spaced out at the start. The smallest ship is two cells, so checking every other cell (like a checkerboard) is enough to guarantee you'll find every ship eventually.
  • Don't forget to finish. If you've hit a ship but don't see the "sunk" message yet, keep firing on adjacent cells — there's still part of that ship floating.

How to play

About this game

Codebreaker is a code-cracking deduction game. One player makes a secret color code; the other tries to guess it from feedback alone. Pencil-and-paper versions of the game (often called Bulls and Cows) have been around for over a century — players took turns trying to guess each other's secret 4-digit numbers and got told how many digits were exactly right and how many were just present-but-misplaced.

The peg-and-board version most people know was invented in 1970 by Mordecai Meirowitz, an Israeli postmaster who couldn't find a publisher willing to take it on at first — until a small British company picked it up. It became one of the best-selling games of the 1970s.

Goal

If you're the codebreaker, crack the hidden color code before you run out of guesses. If you're the codemaker, pick a code that's hard to guess in time.

How a guess works

  1. The codemaker (computer or other player) picks a secret code: 4 colored pegs in a specific order. Colors can repeat.
  2. The codebreaker clicks a color in the palette below, then clicks one of the four empty peg slots in the bottom (active) row to fill it. Click again to overwrite.
  3. When all four slots are filled, click Submit guess.
  4. Feedback appears next to your guess: four colored dots show how close you were.

What the feedback dots mean

  • Green dot — one of your pegs is the right color in the right position.
  • Yellow dot — one of your pegs is the right color, but in the wrong position.
  • Red dot — that peg's color isn't in the secret code at all.

The dots don't tell you which pegs were right — just how many of each kind. Working out which is which is the puzzle. Note: dots are deliberately shown in a fixed order (green first, then yellow, then red), not lined up with your guess pegs.

How duplicates work

The secret code can have repeated colors (like red-red-blue-green). If your guess has two reds and the code only has one red, you'll only get one dot for those two reds — never two. Each peg in the secret code can "match" at most one of your pegs.

Two-player on one device

One player picks the secret code, then a privacy curtain covers the screen while they pass it to the other player. The guesser plays until they crack it or run out of guesses. Then players swap and play a second round — the player who cracks the code in fewer guesses wins overall. (Failing to crack it counts as max guesses + 1 for scoring purposes.)

Playing against the computer

The computer picks a random secret code. Difficulty changes the puzzle size:

  • Easy — 6 colors, 4 pegs, 12 guesses. Most forgiving.
  • Medium — 6 colors, 4 pegs, 10 guesses. The classic ruleset.
  • Hard — 8 colors, 4 pegs, 10 guesses. More colors, same patience.

Tips for your first games

  • Start with a "scout" guess. Try 4 different colors on your first row to learn which colors are in the code.
  • If you get black + white feedback, change positions next. Move the colors that gave white dots to different slots to figure out where they belong.
  • Track what you've ruled out. If your guess of red-red-red-red gets zero dots, no slot is red. That's a huge piece of information.
  • Don't repeat a guess. The button will let you, but you'll just waste a turn — you already know the feedback.
